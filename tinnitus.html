Tinnitus

Tinnitus is the perception of sound—often described as ringing, buzzing, hissing, or humming—in the absence of an external source. It arises from abnormal activity within the auditory system, typically following noise exposure, otologic injury, or neural dysfunction.

While tinnitus and hyperacusis are distinct conditions, they frequently occur together and may share underlying mechanisms such as increased central auditory gain, neural hyperactivity, or damage to cochlear nerve fibers. In many patients, the presence of tinnitus reflects maladaptive changes in how the brain interprets reduced or distorted auditory input, similar to how hyperacusis represents an over-amplified response to external sounds.

The combination of tinnitus and hyperacusis can be particularly distressing—one producing internal noise and the other heightening external sound perception—leading to anxiety, sleep disturbance, and reduced quality of life. Stabilizing the auditory environment, avoiding further acoustic trauma, and supporting the nervous system’s recovery are key components in managing both conditions.

Tinnitus Retraining Therapy

Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT) was developed by Dr. Pawel Jastreboff as a method intended to help individuals habituate to the perception of tinnitus. The approach combines sound therapy—using low-level background noise to reduce the contrast between tinnitus and ambient sound—with counseling aimed at reducing the emotional and attentional impact of tinnitus.

While TRT has been widely promoted and adopted in clinical practice, systematic reviews and high-quality studies have not demonstrated consistent evidence that it provides significant or reliable benefit. Despite its theoretical appeal, there is currently no strong scientific data confirming that TRT effectively reduces tinnitus perception or improves long-term outcomes.

The main components of Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT) are outlined below. TRT typically combines sound enrichment, counseling, and strategies aimed at habituating the brain to tinnitus.

  • Sound Therapy:
    • Uses low-level background noise (often white noise or environmental sounds) to partially mask tinnitus.
    • Reduces the contrast between tinnitus and ambient sound, aiming to make the tinnitus less noticeable.
  • In-Ear Sound Therapy Devices:
    • Small devices worn in the ear that deliver continuous low-level white noise directly into the ear.
    • Designed to provide consistent sound enrichment and facilitate habituation to tinnitus.
  • Counseling / Educational Component:
    • Helps patients understand the auditory system and the mechanisms behind tinnitus.
    • Aims to reduce fear, stress, and negative emotional responses associated with tinnitus.
  • Habituation Goal:
    • Seeks to retrain the brain to gradually ignore the tinnitus signal.
    • Encourages the auditory system to perceive tinnitus as a neutral, non-threatening sound over time.
  • Long-Term, Daily Use:
    • Sound therapy devices are typically worn for many hours per day.
    • Counseling sessions may continue for months to support adherence and cognitive reframing.

Cognitive Behavioral Therapy

While cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is frequently recommended for individuals with tinnitus or hyperacusis, there is currently no convincing evidence demonstrating that it reliably alleviates the core auditory symptoms. CBT may help some patients cope with stress, anxiety, or the emotional distress associated with these conditions, but it does not address the underlying sensory dysfunction. As such, claims that CBT can directly reduce tinnitus perception or sound sensitivity are not supported by high-quality clinical research.

On Dr. Pawel Jastreboff

In 1988, Dr. Jastreboff developed the neurophysiological model of tinnitus and introduced Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT), building on his research into the underlying physiological mechanisms of the condition. His 2004 textbook, Tinnitus Retraining Therapy: Implementing the Neurophysiological Model (co-authored with Jonathan W. P. Hazell), outlined the principles of TRT and helped popularize the neurophysiological model, though its clinical effectiveness remains unsubstantiated by rigorous evidence.

Each team member at Hyperacusis Guide suffers from some degree of hyperacusis, noxacusis, and tinnitus. Three of us are completely homebound and unable to work, drive a car, or even walk outside. In the early stages of our conditions, each of us attempted to follow the principles of Tinnitus Retraining Therapy (TRT). Not only did these methods fail to bring improvement, but in several cases, they appeared to contribute to further worsening. Despite the widespread promotion of TRT within audiology, we believe the approach does not adequately address the complex physiological reality of severe cases and often dismisses the experiences of those most profoundly affected.

In his 2004 book, Tinnitus Retraining Therapy: Implementing the Neurophysiological Model, Dr. Jastreboff suggests that some patients who do not respond to TRT are "inventing or exaggerating" their symptoms to maintain disability benefits. On page 138, the book states the following:

    "Patients involved in litigation, or whose income depends on the continued experience of tinnitus/hyperacusis (i.e., disability payments), typically show very slow progress or no progress at all. Putting aside the possibility of inventing or exaggerating their symptoms because of potential financial gain, these patients are constantly being interviewed by different doctors and lawyers, which brings tinnitus/hyperacusis constantly to their attention, reinforcing its negative aspects, especially feelings of anger and grievance. This works directly against the approach and principles of TRT.

    Subconsciously, the awareness that tinnitus distress may enhance any payments or income/disability received through the legal process can also militate against habituation of tinnitus. It is essential to ask patients about impending litigation and to warn them about the possibility of a negative impact on treatment. When appropriate, it is advisable to counsel patients about the benefits of avoidance or speedy conclusion of legal activity, after which TRT can proceed as in other patients."

In reality, while some individuals with hyperacusis or tinnitus may be eligible for short-term disability benefits through their employer if they become homebound, Social Security Disability Insurance (SSDI) does not recognize these conditions as qualifying disorders for long-term disability benefits. At Hyperacusis Guide, we are not aware of a single case in which an individual has successfully received SSDI benefits for hyperacusis or tinnitus.

Unfortunately, because the severity of hyperacusis and tinnitus cannot be measured objectively with a diagnostic test and relies entirely on the patient’s subjective description, it is impossible to definitively prove or disprove an "expert’s" assessment. This makes it difficult to challenge opinions of "experts"—such as Dr. Jastreboff—as there is no objective standard for comparison.
