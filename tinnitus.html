Tinnitus

Tinnitus is the perception of sound—often described as ringing, buzzing, hissing, or humming—in the absence of an external source. It arises from abnormal activity within the auditory system, typically following noise exposure, otologic injury, or neural dysfunction. While tinnitus and hyperacusis are distinct conditions, they frequently occur together and may share underlying mechanisms such as increased central auditory gain, neural hyperactivity, or damage to cochlear nerve fibers. In many patients, the presence of tinnitus reflects maladaptive changes in how the brain interprets reduced or distorted auditory input, similar to how hyperacusis represents an over-amplified response to external sounds. The combination of tinnitus and hyperacusis can be particularly distressing—one producing internal noise and the other heightening external sound perception—leading to anxiety, sleep disturbance, and reduced quality of life. Stabilizing the auditory environment, avoiding further acoustic trauma, and supporting the nervous system’s recovery are key components in managing both conditions.
