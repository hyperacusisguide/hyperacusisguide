Hyperacusis

Hyperacusis is a condition characterized by an abnormally heightened sensitivity to everyday sounds that are typically well tolerated by others. People with hyperacusis may perceive normal environmental noises (such as running water, clinking dishes, or voices) as uncomfortably loud, intrusive, or even painful. This reaction does not stem from hearing loss but rather from a dysregulation within the auditory system, where sound processing pathways become overly reactive. Hyperacusis may occur alone or alongside tinnitus, noxacusis, or other auditory disorders.

The condition often follows acoustic trauma, prolonged noise exposure, otologic injury, or neurological dysfunction, and its severity can vary widely. For many individuals, hyperacusis causes distress, anxiety, and social withdrawal as they attempt to avoid environments that provoke discomfort. Proper management focuses on protecting against harmful sound exposure while gradually reintroducing tolerable sounds in a controlled, stable environment to help restore auditory tolerance.

Key Features

  • Heightened sensitivity to ordinary environmental sounds.
  • Sounds may feel unusually loud, uncomfortable, or even painful.
  • Can affect one ear (unilateral) or both ears (bilateral).
  • Often occurs alongside tinnitus or noxacusis.
  • May lead to anxiety, stress, or avoidance of noisy environments.
  • Severity varies ─ some individuals experience mild discomfort, while others have debilitating sound sensitivity.
  • Often triggered by acoustic trauma, prolonged noise exposure, or neurological dysfunction.
  • Management includes sound protection and gradual, controlled reintroduction of tolerable sounds.

Noxacusis

Noxacusis is a condition in which everyday sounds are perceived as painful or uncomfortably intense. Unlike hyperacusis, which primarily causes heightened sensitivity, noxacusis often produces a strong, sometimes sharp, physical discomfort in response to ordinary noises. Many individuals with noxacusis also experience hyperacusis, as the two conditions frequently overlap and share similar underlying mechanisms within the auditory system. Noxacusis may also be associated with tonic tensor tympani syndrome (TTTS), a condition in which involuntary contraction of the tensor tympani muscle in the middle ear can amplify the perception of sound and contribute to pain.

Key Features

  • Perception of everyday sounds as painfully loud or uncomfortable.
  • Often co-occurs with hyperacusis, though the discomfort is more acute.
  • May be linked to involuntary contractions of the middle ear muscles, particularly the tensor tympani (TTTS).
  • Can lead to avoidance of noisy environments, anxiety, and social withdrawal.
  • Severity varies widely between individuals, from mild discomfort to debilitating pain.
  • Management typically includes sound protection, controlled exposure to tolerable sounds, and strategies to reduce middle ear muscle overactivity.
  • Understanding the relationship with TTTS is important, as treatment may involve therapies targeting muscle tension in addition controlled sound exposure with gradual desensitization to sound.
  • Minimizing exposure to loud or triggering sounds is essential to prevent worsening of noxacusis.

Misphonia

Hyperacusis and misophonia are both conditions involving abnormal sound sensitivity, but they differ in their underlying nature and triggers.

Hyperacusis is a physical intolerance to everyday sounds that are perceived as uncomfortably loud or even physically distressing. It reflects an increased auditory gain or sensitivity within the hearing system itself.

In contrast, misophonia is characterized by strong emotional or behavioral reactions (such as anger, anxiety, or disgust) to specific “trigger” sounds, like chewing, breathing, or tapping. These reactions are not related to loudness but rather to how the brain interprets certain sounds as highly aversive.

The Loudness Discomfort Level (LDL) test is often used to help distinguish the two conditions. During the test, tones or speech are played at gradually increasing volumes until the individual indicates the sound is too loud. People with hyperacusis typically have significantly lower LDLs (often 70–90 dB or lower) reflecting reduced tolerance for sound intensity. Individuals with misophonia, however, usually have normal LDL results, since their discomfort stems from emotional or contextual reactions rather than physical loudness.

Individuals on the autism spectrum often experience heightened sensory sensitivities, and studies suggest that approximately 12.8% to 35.5% of those with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) may also meet criteria for misophonia.
