Hyperacusis

Hyperacusis is a condition characterized by an abnormally heightened sensitivity to everyday sounds that are typically well tolerated by others. People with hyperacusis may perceive normal environmental noises (such as running water, clinking dishes, or voices) as uncomfortably loud, intrusive, or even painful. This reaction does not stem from hearing loss but rather from a dysregulation within the auditory system, where sound processing pathways become overly reactive. In some cases, there may also be structural changes in the middle ear, and an emerging theory proposes hypermobility of the stapes as a contributing factor.

The condition often follows acoustic trauma, prolonged noise exposure, otologic injury, or neurological dysfunction, and its severity can vary widely. For many individuals, hyperacusis causes distress, anxiety, and social withdrawal as they attempt to avoid environments that provoke discomfort. Proper management focuses on protecting against harmful sound exposure while gradually reintroducing tolerable sounds in a controlled, stable environment to help restore auditory tolerance.

At Hyperacusis Guide, we have researched hundreds of patient cases and spent over a year directly communicating with individuals suffering from hyperacusis, noxacusis, and tinnitus to gather detailed information about their symptoms and experiences. Through this process, we’ve found that the current medical descriptions of hyperacusis and noxacusis lack clarity, and our observations indicate that these conditions appear to exist on the same spectrum.

Hyperacusis is defined as an abnormal sensitivity to everyday sounds, in which normal environmental noises are perceived as uncomfortably loud, intrusive, or even painful. Noxacusis, by definition, refers to pain resulting from sound exposure that exceeds an individual’s tolerance level. In our experience, when hyperacusis becomes severe enough, pain nearly always develops as part of the condition ─ therefore, we see little reason to distinguish these two as separate disorders.

In our view, the key features of hyperacusis include reduced sound tolerance, aural fullness, tinnitus (often reactive), and discomfort or pain in more advanced cases. These symptoms typically worsen after exposure to sounds above the individual’s tolerance threshold. In the earlier and milder stages, pain is often absent, recovery from setbacks is quicker, and sound tolerance usually returns to baseline. However, in severe cases, pain becomes a persistent feature, and any setback can cause a lasting reduction in sound tolerance. Recovery in such cases tends to be slow and difficult, with some individuals experiencing setbacks that appear permanent or last for a year or longer.

Another observation we have made is that some individuals develop sound sensitivity secondary to neuralgia-related pain, and this should not be considered the same disorder as hyperacusis. These patients often report a burning or aching sensation around the ear that can radiate to the face and head. While the pain or burning may make them unusually sensitive to sound, the mechanism differs from hyperacusis. In hyperacusis, discomfort or pain generally localizes to the middle ear (deep to the tympanic membrane), is often a sharp or stabbing pain, and occurs directly in response to sounds that exceed the individual’s tolerance threshold.

Key Features of Hyperacusis

  • Reduced sound tolerance, aural fullness, tinnitus (often reactive), and discomfort or pain in more advanced cases.
  • Can lead to social withdrawal and isolation to avoid sound exposure. Severe cases are usually completely homebound.
  • Severity varies ─ some individuals experience mild discomfort, while others have debilitating sound sensitivity.
  • Often triggered by acoustic trauma, prolonged noise exposure, or neurological dysfunction.
  • Hyperacusis may occur alongside tonic tensor tympani syndrome (TTTS).

Hyperacusis Severity

At Hyperacusis Guide, we recognized that there is currently not a standardized severity scale for hyperacusis. To address this gap, we propose the following severity scale, designed to classify the condition more consistently, support clinical guidance, and enable the collection of more accurate data for research.

Hyperacusis Severity Scale
Mild
  • Can generally function day-to-day with adaptations.
  • Requires hearing protection in louder environments (e.g., restaurants, shopping, public events).
  • Avoids certain activities that are uncomfortably loud.
  • May still manage quiet activities or maintain a quiet job.
Moderate
  • Symptoms interfere significantly with work and daily activities.
  • May have to stop current employment or change to a low-noise job.
  • Often makes a choice to be primarily homebound to not worsen, leaving mainly for doctor visits or necessities, which is something they still have tolerance for.
  • Social activities and travel are very restricted.
  • Hearing protection is needed for almost all situations outside the home.
Severe
  • Unable to tolerate many ordinary sounds, often including normal conversation.
  • Requires hearing protection for most things in and out of the home.
  • Talking, household sounds, and minimal daily activity provoke strong symptoms.
  • Homebound has become absolutely mandatory to not worsen.
  • Those in this category do not leave home at all, or not for very long periods of time, and typically only in life-or-death or extremely pressing situations.
Catastrophic
  • Encompasses everything in the severe category, but at a level beyond in both intensity and susceptibility to worsening.
  • Symptoms can be worsened by things as simple as whispering, eating, running water, or flushing toilets even in protection.

Misphonia

Hyperacusis and misophonia are both conditions involving abnormal sound sensitivity, but they differ in their underlying nature and triggers.

Hyperacusis is a physical intolerance to everyday sounds that are perceived as uncomfortably loud or even physically distressing. It reflects an increased auditory gain or sensitivity within the hearing system itself.

In contrast, misophonia is characterized by strong emotional or behavioral reactions (such as anger, anxiety, or disgust) to specific “trigger” sounds, like chewing, breathing, or tapping. These reactions are not related to loudness but rather to how the brain interprets certain sounds as highly aversive.

The Loudness Discomfort Level (LDL) test is often used to help distinguish the two conditions. During the test, tones or speech are played at gradually increasing volumes until the individual indicates the sound is too loud. People with hyperacusis typically have significantly lower LDLs (often 70–90 dB or lower) reflecting reduced tolerance for sound intensity. Individuals with misophonia, however, usually have normal LDL results, since their discomfort stems from emotional or contextual reactions rather than physical loudness.

Individuals on the autism spectrum often experience heightened sensory sensitivities, and studies suggest that approximately 12.8% to 35.5% of those with autism spectrum disorder (ASD) may also meet criteria for misophonia. However, it is very important to recognize that hyperacusis is distinct condition and should not be confused with misophonia. Many healthcare providers without experience treating hyperacusis may incorrectly assume that hyperacusis sufferers have misophonia.
