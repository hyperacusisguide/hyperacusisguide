Precautionary Guide For New Hyperacusis Patients

Purpose

To stabilize symptoms, prevent worsening, ensure accurate diagnosis and treatment planning, and give the best chance of improvement.

Important Note

This protocol offers cautionary advice based on evidence of proven worsening from sound exposures and aims to support recovery in the best way possible...

1. Controlled, Predictable Sound Environment

  • Create a quiet home base where sound levels are predictable and consistently below your discomfort threshold.
  • Eliminate sudden, unpredictable noises (door slams, kitchen clatter, TV/radio, etc.).
  • Avoid any sound that causes pain, burning, flare-ups, or worsening of symptoms.

2. Protective Equipment Use

  • Use double protection: high-NRR foam earplugs plus quality over-the-ear earmuffs such as Peltor X5A.
  • Keep protection on for the full duration of the trip.
  • Keep spare plugs and muffs accessible at all times.

3. Digital Audio Elimination

  • No headphones, earbuds, or speakers playing music, podcasts, or videos.
  • Use live captioning, text, or email options instead of phone call audio.
  • Limit non-essential media until stability improves.

4. Homebound Voluntary Precaution

  • Minimize leaving home except for essential medical visits.
  • Maintain a low-noise environment through planned errands and deliveries.

5. Diagnostic Imaging

  • Schedule high-resolution CT scan of the temporal bones (≤ 0.6 mm slice thickness) to rule out structural causes.
  • Ensure both sides are imaged.

6. Specialist Review

  • Review imaging results with an ENT, otologist, or neurotologist experienced in relevant procedures.

7. Treatment Planning

  • Discuss possible interventions after imaging.

7.5. Time & Preparedness

  • Improvement takes time and varies by individual. Early preparedness is important.

8. Medication Caution

  • Avoid psychiatric medications unless absolutely necessary.
  • Use benzodiazepines only rarely, short-term if needed.

9. Lifestyle & Mental Health Support

  • Gentle movement practices (yoga, Tai Chi, Qigong).
  • Meditation, prayer, and quiet time in nature.
  • Light-to-moderate exercise within safe sound limits.
  • Healthy diet and stress management.

10. General Precautions

  • Avoid trial-and-error sound therapy.
  • Prioritize rest, sleep, and stress management.
  • Avoid procedures that may worsen your condition (e.g., MRI, dental ultrasonic cleaning).

11. Workplace & Disability Accommodations

  • Explore FMLA, ADA accommodations, disability benefits, and work-from-home options to minimize audio exposure.
Diagnostic Imaging

Diagnostic imaging plays a supportive role in evaluating severe hyperacusis and noxacusis.

1. Purpose of Imaging

  • Rule out secondary causes.
  • Identify structural abnormalities in auditory or neurological pathways.
  • Guide management for atypical, asymmetric, or progressive symptoms.

2. Common Imaging Modalities

a. MRI

  • Preferred for unilateral or neurologically complex cases.
  • Detects vestibular schwannoma, vascular compression, demyelinating disease, brainstem lesions, SSCD.

b. CT

  • Best for bony and middle-ear abnormalities.
  • Detects SSCD, perilymph fistula, otosclerosis, ossicular defects.

c. Functional / Research Imaging

  • fMRI and PET show increased activation in auditory and limbic regions.
  • Supports central gain enhancement and emotional-auditory network dysfunction.

3. Clinical Indications

  • Unilateral or asymmetric symptoms
  • Auditory pain triggered by normal sound levels
  • Associated hearing loss or tinnitus
